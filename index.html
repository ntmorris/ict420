Biography

Caesar was born on July 12th, 100 BC. There isn't much written of his childhood. In 85 BC, his father died unexpectedly. Thus, a young teenaged Caesar became the head of the family. At this time, a civil war had broken out between Sulla1 and Marius2. Which of the two was in power changed repeatedly. Not only was Caesar Marius' nephew by marriage, but Caesar was also married to the daughter of Cinna (Marius' ally). So, while Caesar was not explicitly part of the war, he had heavy connections to very powerful individuals on one side. This happened to be the losing side. Caesar's connections made him a target of Sulla's re-installed regime. Many restrictions were placed on Caesar. Though Sulla reluctantly pardoned him, Caesar thought it best to stay far away, so he joined the military.

Sulla died in 78 BC. Seeing as Sulla was Caesar's reason for being away, he returned to Rome. However, Caesar was left to a modest restart in his life, being that Sulla revoked Caesar's inheritance for his connections to Marius' regime. He took to legal advocacy. Once, while travelling the Aegean Sea3, Caesar was kidnapped by pirates. He was unimpressed with their demand of 20 talents of silver for his ransom and implored them to demand more. He promised them he would have them crucified once freed. Soon, the ransom was paid. Caesar quickly raised a fleet and crucified his former captors.

Not long after, he was called back to military service. Subsequent to his successes, he was elected Military Tribune4. In 69 BC, he was elected Quaestor5, which he served as in the province of Hispania (modern Spain). That same year his aunt and wife both died. Four years later, he was elected Curule Aedile6, then Pontifex Maximus7 in 63 BC. Many accusations were pointed at Caesar during this time, from bribery to being a part of the Catilinarian Conspiracy8.

In trade to Crassus9, Caesar supported Crassus' efforts against Pompey10 as Crassus settled Caesar's debts. Caesar continued his military victories in Hispania, where he was proclaimed Imperator11. He wished for a Triumph12 to be held in his honor, but he was required to be in Rome if to stand for the consul election. Appealing to stand for the election in absence, he was forced to choose the Triumph or the election in ultimatum, which he chose the latter. Caesar was then elected Consul for the year 59 BC.

During his consulship, he had an informal alliance with two others: Crassus and Pompey. This is known as the First Triumvirate13. They were the three most powerful people in Rome. They reconciled their differences for the sake of power. Caesar proposed redistributing public land to the poor of Rome, even if by force. The Triumvirate used force to pass the legislation, putting soldiers in the Roman capital. Caesar secured the governership of Gaul14 (then Southern France) for after his consulship.

Gaul was on the frontier of Rome. What is now France was a land of Gallic tribes with uneasy relations. Over the next several years, Caesar conquered the rest of modern France little by little. Meanwhile, the First Triumvirate broke down. Crassus was killed in battle. Caesar's wife (Pompey's daughter) died in childbirth. Caesar offered marriage to his great-niece to Pompey to regain his favor -- as Caesar was still indebted to him. Pompey declined. He instead married the daughter of one of Caesar's political opponents. Caesar later went on to achieve one of his greatest, and most strategically impressive, victories: Alesia. The victory at which solidified all of modern France into the Roman Republic.

One of Caesar's greatest military feats was the Battle of Alesia in 52 BC. It was extremely unorthodox. His opponent, Vercingetorix, was holding up in a well defended fort on a hill with reinforcements on the way. Seeing that attacking was suicide, and waiting was also suicide, Caesar ordered his men to cut down thousands of trees. They then built an inverted fort, one with the walls facing the fort they were sieging. Then, they cut thousands more trees, building yet another fort facing the conventional direction. Alesia was thus a fort within an inverted fort within a fort. In this battle, a young officer named Mark Antony15, would come to the spotlight and become Caesar's right-hand man.

The Senate ordered Caesar to return to Rome at the end of his tenure as governor of Gaul. He thought that he might be put on trial for his actions as Consul -- because, by Roman law, he was immune to this as governor. He got word that Pompey accused him of insubordination and treason. So, in January of 49 BC, Caesar began his journey to Rome. Per Roman law, it was illegal for armies to cross the Rubicon River16. Knowing the accusations, Caesar crossed with one legion (several thousand men), saying: "The die is cast". Pompey and the senators fled to the South, having little faith in the newly raised troops of Pompey. Pompey escaped to Hispania (Spain). In pursuit, Caesar left Mark Antony in charge of Italy. Caesar decisively defeated Pompey in two battles. Pompey then escaped to Egypt.

Caesar was then appointed Dictator17 in Rome and appointed Mark Antony his second-in-command. Caesar, as dictator, presided over his election to Consul again, then resigned the Dictatorship. Caesar promptly pursued Pompey to Egypt, whereupon he was presented with Pompey's head. He then had Pompey's assassins put to death. Egypt was in a civil war at this time. It became known that the pharoah was the one who had Pompey killed. Likely due to this, Caesar intervened in the Egyptian civil war against the pharoah, won, and installed the pharoah's sister as the new pharoah, Cleopatra, with whom he likely fathered a son, Caesarion. Caesar went on to conquer Pontus, a kingdom on the southern coast of the Black Sea. On his way to which, he stopped in Tarsus (a city in modern Turkey). A supporter of Pompey had planned to assassinate Caesar there. With this realization, after conquering Pontus, Caesar left to Africa, then Spain to defeat the remnants of Pompey's sons supporters. Ending the civil war, Caesar was declared Dictator for ten years.

Caesar used his second tenure as Dictator -- now bestowed for 10 years -- to enact many reforms. He passed a new constitution, which he used for several of his own goals. Slowly, he solidified more and more power to himself, while waning the strength of other institutions, such as the senate. His ambitions grew, and he had a "Forum of Caesar" built in Rome. After many, many years, the Roman calendar had misaligned with the seasons. He reformed the calendar, thus making the Julian Calendar, a descendant calendar is used today. The month of July is named in Caesar's honor.

On the Ides of March (an important date of the Roman calendar), Caesar warily took his seat at the head of the senate floor. One senate approached Caesar to have his brother recalled from exile. Caesar waved him away, but other senators were gathering around Caesar at this moment. The senator which initially approached him pulled his tunic down, exposing Caesar's chest. The gathering senators produced their daggers, stabbing Caesar a total of 23 times. The state of Rome began declining after his assassination. To restore order, the Second Triumvirate was formed. This was made up of: Mark Antony, Octavian18 (whom Caesar adopted), and Lepidus. This, too, broke down. A series of civil wars were then fought, culminating in Octavian's rise to be the first emperor. He proclaimed himself by his new name: Augustus Caesar.
